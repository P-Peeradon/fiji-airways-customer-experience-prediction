# A1 Fijian Grammar & Business Vocabulary Bank

To fully grasp the NLP text processing and the operational culture of Fiji Airways, it is incredibly helpful to have a baseline understanding of the iTaukei (Fijian) language. 

This artifact serves as an "A1 Level" crash course, providing foundational grammar rules and a curated vocabulary bank specifically focused on business, travel, and aviation in Fiji.

---

## 1. Quick Pronunciation Guide (Crucial for reading)
Fijian uses the Latin alphabet but alters five consonants. If you do not know these rules, you will mispronounce almost every location and name in Fiji:
*   **b** sounds like `mb` (*Bula* = mboo-la)
*   **c** sounds like `th` (*Cagi* = tha-ngi)
*   **d** sounds like `nd` (*Nadi* = Nahn-di)
*   **g** sounds like `ng` (like in "singer") (*Sega* = seng-ah)
*   **q** sounds like `ng-g` (like in "finger") (*Yaqona* = yang-go-nah)

## 2. Basic Grammar (A1 Level)

### Sentence Structure (VOS)
Unlike English (Subject-Verb-Object), Fijian naturally places the verb or adjective at the front of the sentence. 
*   **English**: "The food is good."
*   **Fijian**: *"E vinaka na kakana."* (Literally: "Is good the food").
    *   *E* = Predicate marker (acts like "is")
    *   *vinaka* = Good
    *   *na* = The (definite article)
    *   *kakana* = Food

### Negation
If a passenger is unhappy, they will use negation. The primary word for "No" or "Not" is **Sega**. When negating an adjective, you use **sega ni**.
*   *vinaka* = Good
*   *sega ni vinaka* = Not good

### Tense Markers
Unlike English, Fijian verbs do not change form (there is no "-ed" or "-ing" added to words). Instead, tense is indicated by placing a short marker word before the verb.
*   **a** = Past (e.g., *Au a lako* - I went)
*   **sa** = Present state (e.g., *Au sa lako* - I am going / I have gone)
*   **na** = Future (e.g., *Au na lako* - I will go)

### Pronouns & The Complexity of "We"
Fijian pronouns are highly specific. The most crucial concept in business communication is distinguishing between an "inclusive we" and an "exclusive we".
*   **Au**: I / Me
*   **Iko**: You
*   **Koya**: He / She / It
*   **Keda**: We (Inclusive: "You and me") - *Use this when speaking about a shared goal with the customer.*
*   **Keimami**: We (Exclusive: "Me and my team, but not you") - *Use this when speaking about the airline's staff.*

### Articles (Proper vs. Common)
Unlike English, which uses "the" for almost everything, Fijian distinguishes between common nouns and proper nouns (names of people or places).
*   **Na**: The (used for objects/concepts). *Example: Na waqavuka (The airplane).*
*   **O** (or **Ko**): A marker used before names of people or places. *Example: O Nadi (Nadi), O Pita (Peter).*

### Adjectives & Pluralization (Parts of Speech)
*   **Adjective Placement**: Adjectives come *after* the noun they describe. 
    *   *English*: The big airplane.
    *   *Fijian*: Na waqavuka levu (The airplane big).
*   **Plurals**: Nouns do not take an "-s". Plurality is indicated by context or by placing the word **vei** before the noun. *Example: Na vei waqavuka (The airplanes).*

### Possession
For general objects (like luggage or tickets), possession is shown by a specific word placed before the noun:
*   **Noqu**: My. *Example: Na noqu kato (My bag).*
*   **Nona**: His / Her / Its. *Example: Na nona tikite (His/Her ticket).*

## 3. Business & Travel Vocabulary Bank

### The "Big Three" (Essential Etiquette)
These three words form the absolute foundation of all business and customer service interactions in Fiji:
1.  **Bula** (or *Ni sa Bula* for respect): Hello / Welcome. (Literally means "Life" or "Health").
2.  **Vinaka** (or *Vinaka vakalevu*): Thank you / Thank you very much. (Also means "Good").
3.  **Kerekere**: Please. (Used when requesting a favor or service).

### Aviation & Travel
*   **Waqavuka**: Airplane (Literally: *waqa* = boat, *vuka* = fly. A flying boat). *Example: Sa roqo na waqavuka. (The airplane has landed.)*
*   **Mala ni waqavuka**: Airport. *Example: Au sa lako ki na mala ni waqavuka. (I am going to the airport.)*
*   **Vuka**: To fly / Flight. *Example: E vinaka na vuka. (The flight is good.)*
*   **Pasese**: Passenger (English loanword). *Example: E vica na pasese? (How many passengers?)*
*   **Tikite**: Ticket (English loanword). *Example: Oqo na noqu tikite. (Here is my ticket.)*
*   **Kato**: Bag / Luggage / Suitcase. *Example: E biu i vei na noqu kato? (Where is my bag put?)*
*   **Vanua**: Land / Country. *Example: Vinaka vakalevu na nomuni vanua. (Thank you very much for your country/land.)*

### Customer Service & Operations
*   **Cakacaka**: Work / Business / Job. *Example: Au sa lako i cakacaka. (I am going to work.)*
*   **Veivuke**: Help / Assistance. *Example: Au kerekere veivuke. (I am asking for help.)*
*   **Totolo**: Fast / Quick. *Example: E totolo na waqavuka. (The airplane is fast.)*
*   **Berabera**: Slow / Delayed. *Example: Sa berabera na vuka. (The flight is delayed.)*
*   **Ca**: Bad / Broken. *Example: E ca na kakana. (The food is bad.)*
*   **Duka**: Dirty. *Example: E duka na noqu idabedabe. (My seat is dirty.)*
*   **Savavasa**: Clean. *Example: E savavasa na waqavuka. (The airplane is clean.)*
*   **Lavo / Ilovo**: Money. *Example: E vica na kena isau ilovo? (How much money does it cost?)*
*   **Sau / Isau**: Price / Cost. *Example: E sau levu oqo. (This price is high / expensive.)*

### Everyday Communication & Basics
*   **Io**: Yes. *Example: Io, au sa kila. (Yes, I understand.)*
*   **Sega**: No. *Example: Sega, au sega ni kila. (No, I don't understand.)*
*   **Yacaqu o...**: My name is... *Example: Na yacaqu o John. (My name is John.)*
*   **Vacava tiko?**: How are you? *Example: Bula, vacava tiko? (Hello, how are you?)*
*   **Bulabula vinaka**: I am well / healthy. *Example: Au bulabula vinaka tiko. (I am doing well.)*
*   **Kakana**: Food. *Example: E vinaka na kakana. (The food is good.)*
*   **Wai**: Water. *Example: Kerekere dua na bilo wai. (Please, a cup of water.)*
*   **Vale**: House / Building. *Example: Oqo na noqu vale. (This is my house.)*
*   **Lako**: To go / Walk. *Example: Au sa lako. (I am going.)*
*   **Mai**: Come (or "from"). *Example: Au mai Nadi. (I am from Nadi.)*
*   **Lako mai**: Come here. *Example: Lako mai kike. (Come here.)*

### Time & Scheduling
*   **Siga**: Day. *Example: E dua na siga vinaka. (It is a good day.)*
*   **Siga nikua**: Today. *Example: Au na vuka e na siga nikua. (I will fly today.)*
*   **Mataka**: Tomorrow / Morning. *Example: Ni sa yadra, mataka vinaka. (Good morning.)*
*   **Yakavi**: Afternoon. *Example: Bula yakavi. (Good afternoon.)*
*   **Bogi**: Night. *Example: Ni sa moce, bogi vinaka. (Good night.)*
*   **Vula**: Month. *Example: Oqo na vula o Me. (This is the month of May.)*
*   **Yabaki**: Year. *Example: Yabaki vou vinaka! (Happy New Year!)*

## 4. Applying this to the Business Context
In a Singaporean business context, a transaction is often purely transactional and efficiency-based. In Fiji, business is deeply relational. 

If a Fiji Airways flight attendant says **"Bula"** to a passenger, it is not just a robotic greeting; it is an active offering of life and hospitality. When parsing our NLP data, understanding that a word like **Vinaka** means both "Good" and "Thank you" highlights how deeply gratitude and quality are intertwined in the Fijian business mindset.
